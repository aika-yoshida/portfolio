# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Recruiters and hiring managers evaluating a career change into UX/CX design. They arrive with limited time, skim quickly, and are deciding whether to move the candidate to an interview. Most review on desktop during working hours; some will click through on mobile first.

## Product Purpose

A portfolio for Aika Yoshida, a former B2B sales professional now working as a web designer and studying UX/CX. Its job is to get her contacted for interviews by proving she can reason through a design problem, not just execute a polished screen. Each featured case study walks 課題 → 分析 → 施策 explicitly, and the About section draws a straight line from her sales background to her design habits. Success is a recruiter emailing her after reading two or three case studies.

## Positioning

法人営業で鍛えた課題発見力で、見た目より先に「なぜ」を問うデザインをする。

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: email (`mailto:aika.yos@icloud.com`), present in the topbar and the closing contact panel.
- Secondary CTA (planned, not yet built): resume/CV PDF download and a LinkedIn profile link, offered alongside email for recruiters who prefer to forward internally or reach out via LinkedIn.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: 課題を解くことから、体験の設計へ — a sales background that spots the real problem before touching the screen.
- Belief ladder: Credible (this is real work with real constraints, not a student exercise) → Capable (she can actually reason through a UX problem end to end) → Worth the risk (the non-traditional sales background is an asset, not a gap to explain away).
- Proof on hand: four detailed case studies (patient record system, wedding venue redesign, 東都典範 site renewal, Penelope a casa) each with role, timeframe, and process detail; two include a downloadable proposal PDF, referenced inline via the `.pdf` tag on the works cards.

## Brand Personality

Calm, precise, considered. The voice is honest and direct rather than persuasive — it distinguishes 実務経験 (hands-on experience) from 学習・実践中 (currently learning) instead of blurring the two for effect. Purple is the one accent color against an ink/cream editorial base; it marks emphasis, not decoration. Confidence comes from specificity (numbers, roles, timeframes) rather than claims of expertise.

## Anti-references

Generic SaaS-style card grids with identical icon+heading+text tiles. Glassmorphism used decoratively. Over-animated UI that calls attention to itself instead of the work. Also avoid the broader AI-portfolio tells: gradient text, tiny tracked eyebrows above every section, hero-metric templates.

## Design Principles

Show the reasoning, not just the result — every case study earns its place by walking a real problem through analysis to a decision, not by ending on a pretty final screen. Disclose honestly rather than polish over gaps — real experience and areas still being learned are labeled separately, because that honesty is itself the credibility signal. Restraint over volume — one accent color, one structural idea per section, editorial pacing instead of loud selling; quiet and considered beats bold and generic. Specificity beats adjectives — numbers, role, timeframe, and named tools stand in for claims like "results-driven" or "user-centered." The sales background is an asset to state plainly, not an apology folded into the copy.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline: verified contrast ratios, visible focus states, 44px minimum touch targets, and `prefers-reduced-motion` support are already in place across the shared design tokens and should be preserved in any new page or component.
